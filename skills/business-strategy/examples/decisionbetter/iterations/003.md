## Research Hypothesis

I am researching (1) the fractional AI officer model because I need to validate whether it is a meaningfully different and higher-value offering than the current DFY/retainer options, and (2) Microsoft Copilot partnership because I need to validate whether technology reselling creates a viable revenue stream alongside consulting.

**Contrarian check** (first since iteration 0): What would a brilliant contrarian do in this market? What is everyone else missing?

**Key findings:**
- Fractional AI Officer is a distinct, higher-value positioning vs. DFY implementer — commands $5K–$10K/month vs. $1.5K–$3.5K retainer
- Microsoft Copilot partnership creates a recurring revenue layer but has high compliance/setup cost and thin margins
- AIWise already offering Fractional CTO — validates demand but reduces differentiation

**Contrarian analysis:**
Everyone in AU SMB AI consulting is racing to serve the "AI-ready" buyer (the 37% who have "tried AI"). The contrarian insight: **the 63% who haven't tried AI at all are actually easier to convert** because they have NO prior failure experience and are approaching with fresh minds. The challenge is they have lower willingness to pay initially — but if the first engagement is a $500–$1,500 "taster" workshop, they convert to full buyers later. The contrarian bet: own the "AI skeptic SMB" category before competitors flood the "AI-curious" segment.

### Option K: Budget AI Taster for Skeptics (Contrarian Bet)
- **What**: Create a super-accessible entry product: "AI in 3 Hours" — a 3-hour on-site workshop for businesses that are skeptical or new to AI. Priced at AUD $750–$1,200 (all-inclusive, fixed price, no discovery call needed). Positioned as a "no-risk trial run." Easy to market via targeted Facebook/Instagram ads to business owner audiences. Volume play.
- **Why it might work (contrarian logic)**: Everyone else is competing for the 37% of SMBs who have already "tried" AI. The 63% who haven't are being ignored because the sales cycle is perceived as harder. But a $997 no-commitment taster has almost no sales resistance — the buyer's question shifts from "should I invest in AI consulting?" to "is $997 worth 3 hours of my time?" This is a completely different buying decision. These clients, once converted via taster, are excellent DFY prospects because they've had a positive first experience with DecisionBetter specifically.
- **Tags**: Near-term (0–6 months) | Segments: 1, 4
- **Obvious risks**: Low ticket price means low per-client revenue; at $1K per booking, needs volume; may attract price-sensitive buyers who don't convert to higher-value services; could cheapen brand if positioned incorrectly

**Counter-argument**: The risk of brand dilution is real if the taster is positioned as "cheap." But if positioned as "the right starting point for any business curious about AI" — not as a discount — it creates a clear funnel: Taster ($1K) → Workshop ($4.5K) → DFY ($30K). This is the standard consulting land-and-expand model applied to the skeptic segment.

## Key Insight from This Iteration

**The Fractional AI Officer is the strategic evolution of DecisionBetter's current model.** The DFY service currently ends at project completion — leaving both money and relationship on the table. The Fractional AI Officer retainer transforms DecisionBetter from a project vendor into a strategic partner. At $6K/month, three fractional clients generate $18K/month MRR — equivalent to running three full DFY projects every month — but with a fraction of the delivery effort. This is the business model architecture fix that the diagnosis identified as root cause #1.

**The Contrarian insight is real but secondary**: The budget taster (Option K) captures the skeptic segment but generates low individual revenue. Its real value is as a **funnel** feeding into higher-value offerings, not as a standalone strategy.
