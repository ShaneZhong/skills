# Review of Iteration 004

## Verdict: challenged

The strategy is directionally sound but built on softer evidence than it admits, contains a potentially fabricated statistic, and underestimates three structural risks: the AI fatigue cycle, the solo-operator capacity trap, and the speed at which embedded AI tools are commoditising the bottom of the consulting market. The top-ranked option (Productized Workshop Ladder) is reasonable but its WWHTBT "validation" overstates confidence -- all four conditions were validated using informed opinion, not hard data. That is not validation. That is hypothesis confirmation.

## Framing check

**Is the Strategist solving the right problem?**
Partially. The diagnosis correctly identifies the time-for-money trap and the lack of a scalable acquisition system. But the strategic question -- "build acquisition system vs. productize?" -- may be a false choice. The real strategic question that is not being asked is: **"Should DecisionBetter even exist as a standalone consulting business, or should Shane join/partner with an existing firm that already has deal flow?"** A solo operator with no online presence, no reviews, no referral network, and no content engine is trying to build all of this from scratch while also delivering. That is a 60-80 hour/week proposition for 6+ months before seeing meaningful results.

**Are there higher-leverage strategic questions being ignored?**
Yes:
1. Should Shane acquire a co-founder or merge with a complementary business (e.g., an accountancy firm's digital division, a small agency) rather than building solo?
2. Should the first move be getting 3-5 case studies with measurable ROI numbers rather than productizing? You cannot sell an "outcome guarantee" product if you have no public proof the outcomes have been delivered.
3. Is AI consulting the right vehicle at all, or would a SaaS product or AI-as-a-service play be higher leverage?

**Would a different framing lead to different options?**
Yes. If the question were "How does Shane get to $20K/month in recurring revenue within 12 months?" the answer might be "get 3 fractional AI officer clients through direct outreach" rather than "build a productized workshop ladder and hope the funnel fills."

## Bias check

**Anchoring**: Yes. Option A (Productized Workshop Ladder) was introduced in iteration 1 and has dominated every subsequent iteration. The "stress test" in iteration 4 was confirmatory, not adversarial -- it searched for reasons productization works, then searched for reasons it does not, and concluded it works. A genuine stress test would have started with the counter-hypothesis and tried to prove THAT.

**Confirmation**: Strong. The WWHTBT conditions were all "validated" using informed opinion sources (blog posts, consultancy marketing pages), not primary data. The Strategist found what it was looking for. The counter-research was acknowledged but always resolved with a mitigation, never with "this is a real problem that might kill the option."

**Groupthink**: Moderate. All 12 options are variations on the same theme: Shane sells AI consulting services to Australian SMBs. No option considers a fundamentally different business model (e.g., building a product, acquiring a business, becoming an employee of an existing firm, creating a marketplace). The options are different tactics within the same strategy.

**Recency**: Yes. The entire strategy is premised on the AI adoption wave continuing to create demand for AI consultants. The AI fatigue evidence from late 2025 / early 2026 -- where 95% of pilots fail and 42% of initiatives are scrapped -- is not incorporated. The market may be entering a disillusionment trough, not a growth phase.

**Score inflation**: Option A's composite of 4.36 includes a Feasibility score of 5 ("current capability; <30h setup investment"). But feasibility should account for whether the business can generate demand for the productized offering, not just whether it can build a website. Building the product pages is trivial. Getting anyone to visit them is the hard part. Feasibility of 5 is inflated by at least 1 point.

## Factual accuracy

**Critical error: The "91% of SMBs not AI-enabled" statistic is wrong.** The business_context.md attributes this to "Deloitte 2025" but the actual Deloitte November 2025 report states that two-thirds of SMBs ARE using AI. The real non-adoption figure is approximately 33%, not 91%. The "5% fully enabled" statistic (from Scalesuite, not Deloitte) is being conflated with "91% not using AI." The correct framing would be: "~37% have adopted AI, but only 5% are fully enabled" -- meaning 95% are not fully enabled, but that is very different from "91% not using AI." This error inflates the perceived addressable market.

**The "85%+ workshop completion rate vs. industry 5-15%" claim** (business_context.md) is unverified. No source is cited for the industry benchmark. Most corporate training completion rates are actually 20-30% for online self-paced courses. In-person workshops typically have 80-95% completion rates because attendees have already committed a day. Comparing in-person to online completion rates is misleading.

**The $44B Deloitte figure** checks out -- this is from the November 2025 Deloitte Access Economics report commissioned by Amazon.

**The "44% of consultants now productize" claim** is sourced from ConsultingSuccess.com and assembly.com, which are marketing sites for consulting productization tools. This is not independent research; the sources have a commercial interest in promoting productization.

## Blind spots

1. **Zero social proof problem.** DecisionBetter has no reviews on Clutch, GoodFirms, Google, or any directory. No case studies are published. No testimonials are visible. The strategy focuses on productizing the offer and building a funnel, but ignores the fact that a buyer who lands on the website will see zero evidence that anyone has ever used this service. This is a deal-breaker for any SMB spending $2,500-$4,500. The first priority should be getting 3-5 published case studies with named (or industry-identified) clients and measurable outcomes.

2. **AI fatigue and the disillusionment trough.** The strategy assumes growing demand for AI consulting. But MIT, TechCrunch, and IT Pro all report that AI fatigue is real and businesses are becoming skeptical of AI promises. 42% of AI initiatives were scrapped in 2025. The window for "AI is exciting, let me hire a consultant" may be closing. The window for "AI has failed us, we need someone to fix it" may be opening -- but that requires a different positioning than "AI as a coworker."

3. **Microsoft Copilot commoditisation is accelerating faster than acknowledged.** Copilot Business launched December 2025 at $18/user/month for SMBs, no seat minimums, no programming required for agent creation. This directly threatens the bottom of the funnel (the $997 taster). If a $997 taster session is "identify 3 AI opportunities for your business," many SMBs will discover those opportunities are already available in Copilot for $18/month per user. The consulting market is being squeezed from below.

4. **The founder capacity problem is acknowledged but not solved.** The strategy discusses it as a future concern but the productized workshop ladder does not fix it -- Shane still has to deliver every workshop personally. At 2-3 workshops/month plus DFY work, the capacity ceiling is hit within 3 months of any successful demand generation. The strategy has no plan for what happens when the funnel works.

5. **No competitor is doing what DecisionBetter proposes, which could mean either white space or no demand.** The strategy interprets the absence of productized AI workshop competitors in Sydney as an opportunity. The alternative interpretation: the market has been tested by others and found wanting. AIWise's $990 discovery session is the closest analogue, and they have no public reviews either -- suggesting the entire AU SMB AI consulting market may be smaller than the TAM estimates suggest.

6. **Geographic constraint is under-discussed.** The strategy is for in-person workshops in Sydney. This limits the TAM to ~7,000 businesses (per the customer segments doc). At a 0.5% conversion rate, that is 35 clients -- and a 0.5% conversion rate for a consultant with no online presence and no reviews is optimistic.

## Specific challenges

1. **Fix the 91% statistic.** The business context states "91% of SMBs still not AI-enabled (Deloitte 2025)" but Deloitte's actual figure is approximately 33% non-adoption (two-thirds are using AI). This error underpins the TAM estimates and the urgency narrative. It must be corrected immediately, and any downstream analysis that depends on it must be revised.

2. **Address the social proof gap before productizing.** The strategy puts productization first and social proof second. This is backwards. No rational SMB buyer will spend $2,500-$4,500 on a workshop from a consultant with no reviews, no case studies, and no visible track record. The first sprint should be: get 3 workshops delivered (even at a discount), document measurable outcomes, publish case studies, and get 2-3 Google/Clutch reviews. Then productize.

3. **Incorporate the AI fatigue/disillusionment data.** The strategy assumes demand for AI consulting is growing. The evidence from late 2025 suggests it may be entering a correction. The Strategist must either (a) show why DecisionBetter is positioned to benefit from disillusionment (e.g., "we fix failed AI projects") or (b) acknowledge the demand risk and adjust the timeline.

4. **Stress-test the $997 taster against Copilot.** Microsoft Copilot Business is $18/user/month, requires no programming, and is already embedded in the software most SMBs use. What does the $997 taster offer that a SMB cannot get from spending 3 hours with Copilot? If the answer is "we identify workflow automation opportunities beyond basic Copilot," then the taster needs to be positioned above Copilot, not as a generic AI introduction.

5. **Model the capacity constraint explicitly.** If Option A succeeds and generates 3 workshops/month plus DFY upsells, Shane is working at capacity within 90 days. The strategy has no plan for this. What happens? Does Shane (a) raise prices, (b) hire a contractor, (c) create waitlists, or (d) burn out? This needs an explicit decision, not a deferral to "Option J at 6-24 months."

6. **Challenge the "outcome guarantee" at scale.** The outcome guarantee is positioned as a key differentiator. But only 25% of AI initiatives deliver expected ROI, and only 29% of executives can measure ROI. How will DecisionBetter measure and guarantee outcomes when the market itself cannot agree on how to measure AI ROI? What specific outcomes are guaranteed? "Measurable time savings" is vague. This guarantee will either be (a) meaningless because it is vague, or (b) expensive because it is specific. The Strategist must clarify which.

7. **Consider the "join don't build" alternative.** A solo operator with deep AI skills, strong facilitation ability, and a non-technical communication style is a valuable asset. The strategy assumes Shane should build a standalone consulting business from zero. The alternative -- joining an established consultancy, agency, or tech company as a senior practitioner or practice lead -- would provide immediate revenue, deal flow, and credibility, with far less risk. This option has never been evaluated and should be, even if only to explicitly rule it out.

8. **Validate that industry associations actually want AI speakers.** Option H (Industry Association Speaking) scores 3.86 and is recommended for near-term execution. But no evidence has been gathered that any specific association has an open speaking slot, wants AI content, or would accept a speaker from a brand with no public presence. The Strategist should make 3 actual inquiries before scoring this option.

9. **Re-examine whether the funnel architecture is premature.** The strategy has designed a complete five-stage funnel (Awareness -> Entry -> Core -> High Value -> Recurring) before the business has a single review, a single case study, or a proven acquisition channel. Designing funnels is a common procrastination pattern in solo consulting. The next move should be the simplest possible action that generates revenue and evidence: deliver 3 workshops, document results, get reviews. Everything else follows.

10. **Acknowledge what DecisionBetter is actually competing with.** The competitor analysis covers boutique AI consultants. But the real competition for the $997-$4,500 buyer is not AIWise or Wilson AI. It is: (a) doing nothing, (b) asking ChatGPT directly, (c) hiring a freelancer on Upwork for $500, (d) attending a free webinar, or (e) letting Microsoft Copilot handle it. The strategy needs to articulate why a busy SMB owner would choose an unknown consultant over these alternatives.
