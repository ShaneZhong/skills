# Diagnosis: DecisionBetter
*Written: March 2026 | Phase 0*

---

## Root Causes

### Root Cause 1: Revenue model requires founder's time — no path to scale
The current dual offering (workshops + Done For You) both trade founder time for money. Even at premium rates (AUD $3K–$6K/workshop day, $15K–$50K DFY projects), a solo founder can serve 2–4 clients per month maximum. The business has no recurring revenue, no leverage, and no mechanism to grow revenue without proportionally growing workload. This is not a positioning problem or a demand problem — it is a **business model architecture problem**.

### Root Cause 2: Sales pipeline is undefined and founder-dependent
Current clients were acquired through "network and direct outreach." This is unsustainable at scale: Shane's network has finite reach, personal outreach does not compound, and there is no content, referral, or inbound engine generating leads. The business has no predictable top-of-funnel. Revenue is lumpy and reactive rather than planned.

### Root Cause 3: Market is moving faster than positioning
The Australian SMB AI market is bifurcating: (a) trivial AI adoption (Copilot, Canva AI — handle themselves) and (b) serious workflow integration (multi-agent systems, process automation — requires specialist help). DecisionBetter's current positioning ("AI coworker" / workshop + DFY) is strong but may not be specific enough to own a clear category as competition grows. Competitors like AIWise are beginning to productize more specifically. DecisionBetter risks being positioned as "another AI consultant" if it doesn't stake out a more defensible category.

## Value Chain Map

**Where value is created:**
1. **Diagnosis/discovery** — identifying which workflows actually benefit from AI (high expertise required; most SMBs can't do this alone)
2. **Workshop delivery** — translating AI capability into team behaviour change (hands-on, real data, non-technical — genuine differentiation)
3. **DFY implementation** — building actual AI systems in client workflows (high value, high margin if productized)
4. **Ongoing support/optimization** — recurring value extraction from initial build (currently unexploited recurring revenue opportunity)

**Where value is destroyed:**
1. **Sales cycle** — custom discovery calls for every client, no productized entry point → long sales cycle, low conversion efficiency
2. **Delivery capacity** — linear time-for-money model → every new client costs founder time → no leverage
3. **Marketing** — no content engine, no compounding inbound channel → CAC is high (time spent on outreach)

**Biggest untapped value**: Recurring revenue from AI system maintenance, optimization, and expansion after initial implementation. Currently delivered as part of DFY with "ongoing support" but not priced as a retainer.

## Capabilities Assessment

**Strengths (what DecisionBetter is genuinely good at):**
- Deep AI/ML technical knowledge (rare: most SMB consultants lack implementation depth)
- Plain-English communication of complex AI concepts (critical for non-technical buyers)
- Workshop facilitation — adapting training to real client workflows and data
- Implementation: actually building and deploying AI systems (not just advising)
- Outcome guarantee — willingness to stand behind results (signals genuine confidence)

**Gaps (must be addressed to scale):**
- Sales/BD: no systematic pipeline, no CRM, no content funnel
- Marketing: no content-at-scale capability, no SEO presence, no thought leadership yet
- Delivery capacity: cannot serve more than ~3–4 clients/month as solo operator
- Recurring revenue model: no productized retainer or subscription mechanism
- Pricing strategy: no public pricing; unclear whether current rates are optimally positioned

**Critical gap for scale**: The business needs a scalable customer acquisition mechanism that does not require Shane's direct time for every lead. This is prerequisite to any other growth strategy.

---

## The Strategic Question

> **Should DecisionBetter build a scalable acquisition system around its existing workshop/DFY model, OR should it pivot to a productized service model (fixed-scope, fixed-price deliverables) that enables volume growth?**

This is a genuine fork:
- **Path A** (Acquisition System): Keep current offerings, invest in content marketing / SEO / referral partnerships to generate consistent leads. Revenue grows with clients won.
- **Path B** (Productization): Redesign offerings as fixed-scope packages (e.g., "AI Workflow Audit: $2,500 / 5-day turnaround") that can be marketed, sold, and delivered without custom scoping. Revenue grows with volume.

These paths are not mutually exclusive, but the order matters. Attempting both simultaneously risks doing neither well.

**Secondary strategic question**: Should DecisionBetter pursue geographic expansion (Australia-wide via remote/virtual delivery) or own Sydney deeply first before expanding?

## Competitive Positioning

**The two dimensions that matter most to SMB buyers:**
1. **Ease of buying** (discovery-to-start friction): How hard is it to understand, price, and commit?
2. **Depth of implementation** (hands-on vs. advisory): Do you get a working system or a roadmap?

| | Low Implementation Depth | High Implementation Depth |
|---|---|---|
| **High Friction to Buy** | Enterprise consultancies (Deloitte, Protiviti) | Wilson AI (custom scope, technical) |
| **Low Friction to Buy** | Digital agencies adding AI modules (Arcadian) | **DecisionBetter target zone** |

DecisionBetter's target zone: **low friction + high implementation depth** — easy to buy (workshop as first step, transparent outcome guarantee) and actually hands-on (real client data, working systems).

**Current position**: Moderate friction (discovery call required, no public pricing) + high implementation depth. The opportunity is to move toward lower friction without sacrificing depth.

**AIWise comparison**: AIWise has moved toward lower friction ($990 transparent entry point) but may sacrifice depth (builds FOR clients rather than WITH them, creating dependency). DecisionBetter's workshop model builds client capability — potentially a stronger retention mechanism.

## Key Strategic Risks

1. **Commoditisation risk**: As AI becomes easier to use (Microsoft Copilot, HubSpot AI), the addressable market for "basic AI adoption" consulting shrinks. Must move up the complexity curve.
2. **Solo operator capacity ceiling**: Every client acquisition is meaningless if it cannot be served. Scale requires either productization or hiring.
3. **Competitor productization**: AIWise and others are rapidly productizing. If they reach critical review mass on Clutch/GoodFirms before DecisionBetter, they capture the SEO and social proof advantage.
4. **Referral dependency**: "Network and direct outreach" is not a business model. One dry period in Shane's network = revenue drought.
